FROM MY REAL SHIFTS

7 SERVICE-LEARNING FIELD NOTES

Notes from my actual sessions with Alex. I wrote these after my shifts, not during class.

1

Explaining a concept vs. applying it

Early on, Alex could explain what a loop was, but could not use one in a problem. Alex knew the words but froze when it was time to code. That gap between knowing and doing stuck with me.

WHY IT WORKED: a concept is generalized from specific examples (Slavin, 2024, Ch. 7). Alex needed to build a loop, not recite one, so we worked from examples until Alex could apply it (Ambrose et al., 2010).

2

Breaking down a large problem

Another week, Alex was overwhelmed by a programming assignment and didn't know how to begin. Instead of focusing on the code immediately, we broke the problem into smaller steps and wrote out the logic first. Once Alex had a plan, writing the code became much easier.

WHY IT WORKED: planning in plain English first cut the cognitive load (Sweller, 1988), and the outline became a scaffold inside their zone of proximal development (Vygotsky, 1978).

3

Debugging code

While debugging, Alex kept changing random parts of the code hoping it would work. We slowed down and tested one thing at a time. This helped Alex see the importance of using a systematic process rather than guessing.

WHY IT WORKED: testing one change at a time turned each result into formative feedback, evidence about what to fix next instead of a guess (Black & Wiliam, 1998).

4

Reading error messages

Alex used to ignore compiler and Python error messages because they looked intimidating. We spent time reading the messages together and figuring out what they meant. Over time, Alex got much more comfortable using those messages to solve problems independently.

WHY IT WORKED: reading the error together made it usable feedback: where am I, and where to next. That is the engine of self-regulated learning (Hattie & Timperley, 2007; Nicol & Macfarlane-Dick, 2006).

5

Explaining their thought process

Instead of jumping in whenever Alex got stuck, I'd ask Alex to explain what they were thinking. Many times, Alex would catch their own mistake just by talking it through. It showed me that asking questions can sometimes be more effective than giving answers.

WHY IT WORKED: talking it through sparked self-explanation and the fix surfaced on its own. That's scaffolding within the ZPD. The thinking stays with the learner (Vygotsky, 1978).

6

Connecting new concepts to old ones

When Alex was learning a new topic, I connected it to concepts Alex already understood. When we hit functions, I related it back to code Alex had already written. That made the new material feel less intimidating and easier to understand.

WHY IT WORKED: tying functions back to code Alex had already written activated prior knowledge, so the new topic had something to attach to (Bransford et al., 2000; Ambrose et al., 2010).

7

Watching growth over multiple weeks

Alex started the semester needing a push on almost every problem. By midterms, Alex was trying things before asking me. Slow change, but real.

WHY IT WORKED: learning is a change caused by experience (Slavin, 2024, Ch. 4). Every owned win built the confidence to keep going, and the support I gave faded as Alex grew (Bandura, 1997; Vygotsky, 1978).

CH. 4 · WHAT LEARNING IS

“Learning is usually defined as a change in an individual caused by experience.”

tap to flip →

WITH ALEX

I stopped measuring sessions by whether the assignment got done. The real win was whether Alex could do something today that Alex could not do last week (Slavin, 2024, Ch. 4).

CH. 4 · REINFORCEMENT

“Reinforcers increase the frequency of a behavior.”

tap to flip →

WITH ALEX

When I noticed Alex out loud for reading an error instead of guessing, Alex did it more. I reinforced the habit I wanted, not just the right answer (Slavin, 2024, Ch. 4).

CH. 4 · FEEDBACK

“Learning depends on experience, including feedback from the environment.”

tap to flip →

WITH ALEX

Error messages were feedback Alex was ignoring. Once we read them as information instead of insults, Alex could self-correct without me (Slavin, 2024, Ch. 4).

CH. 7 · TEACHING CONCEPTS

“A concept is an abstract idea that is generalized from specific examples.”

tap to flip →

WITH ALEX

I never defined “loop” cold. I showed Alex three small loops first, then let Alex tell me what they had in common. The concept came from the examples (Slavin, 2024, Ch. 7).

CH. 9 · BELONGING

“Creating a sense of belonging.”

tap to flip →

WITH ALEX

I learned Alex's name, asked how the week went, and never acted shocked at a question. Alex kept coming back because the table felt safe (Slavin, 2024, Ch. 9).

CH. 3 · MORAL REASONING

“Children differ in their moral reasoning.”

tap to flip →

WITH ALEX

When Alex wanted to copy a friend's code, I did not lecture. We talked through why it mattered for Alex's own learning. Different students need that conversation pitched differently (Slavin, 2024, Ch. 3).
